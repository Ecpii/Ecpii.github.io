4-Wide

An infamous, but effective, winning strategy.

In the garbage table shown before in multi, you may have noticed that the garbage table goes all the way to 20 in combo, and the garbage amounts increase with combo. This can give your attacks tremendous combo power, since all the attacks will add up and be sent to your opponent. As shown, the power of combos is nothing to scoff at in this game; after 13 combos you have sent 20 lines of garbage, the full height of the board, and bound to kill unless your opponent cancels part of the attack.

Thus, a stacking strategy was created to abuse the combo system: 4-Wide. In this stacking strategy, you build a gap that is four minos wide (as implied in the name), and leave 3 residue minos in the well:

This has been shown to be an optimal way to secure combo, since there are many patterns in which you can place pieces to continually clear lines.

Also, as shown in the video, you could possibly have garbage lines under the 4-Wide which will benefit even more from the combo bonus. A 4-combo quad does double the damage with no combo, and 8 combo does triple, 12 combo quadruple, etc.

Center 4-Wide

There are two main variations of 4-Wide and this is the one generally considered more powerful. This requires you to build your gap in the center, which makes the sides harder to stack since you only have 3 minos of space on each side. Thus, this is generally used in more higher level play/with higher level players.

However, it boasts many advantages. The most major of which is that you are less prone to topping out. Even if the opponent sends you lots of garbage to get the rest of your stack above the 20th line, you still won't have covered your piece spawn position, since the middle of your stack is a big gap:

A consequence of this is that your stack is virtually larger, since you could get lucky and continue your combo through the garbage after you have exhausted your 4-Wide.

Another advantage is that you can place your pieces faster than the other variation since your pieces are already spawning in the gap, so you don't need to move them as far.

But again, this type of stacking requires more thinking because you only have 3 minos of space to work with on each side, and therefore must actively consider the pieces in the next queue in order to stack quickly.

Side 4-Wide

Because Center 4-Wide isn't very easy to build, some players may choose to instead build the 4-wide gap on the side of the board. This forsakes all the advantages of c4w for ease of building.

Using this strategy, you are very prone to dying, since just a couple lines of garbage will shift your stack up to cover piece spawn positions. Additionally, you have to shift all your pieces to the side before you can place them, making your attack pressure much slower.

For some players, this slow down in attack may be worth the faster and easier stacking. However, the main factor is usually still the risk in getting topped-out while building, and most people eventually shift to using c4w.

General Notes

Despite its power, and likely because of its power, most people look down on using 4-Wide. It is said to make the gameplay very stale, since it is just executing the same patterns to maintain combo throughout the game. It is also hated because of its unmatched power; many players have said that the only way to beat four-wide consistently is to use four-wide against it. Fight fire with fire.

It also allows perhaps less-skilled players to beat higher-skilled players, so many look at it like its a crutch, a substitute for skill.
